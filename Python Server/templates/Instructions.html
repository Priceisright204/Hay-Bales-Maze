Instructions

A hay-bales maze is an open maze without walls. Instead of turning where you would like, you must continue straight until you hit a wall. Follow the grid lines closely. In this computer version, the lines are tracked for you. When you hit a wall, you may turn depending on what walls are around you.

Follow the grid squares closely and continue until you hit a wall. If you hit a wall, the turns available to you depend on what other walls are next to you.

I recommend loading the sample maze and attempting it as you read these instructions (Main Menu-->Load Maze-->Load Sample). Then, enter the Maze Solver (Main Menu-->Maze Solver) to solve the maze.

Then, open up the maze creator (Main Menu-->Maze Creator) and make your own maze!

A sample maze with obstacles

Here is a maze. Let me walk through the obstacles one at a time.

1.     This dark green line is the beginning. You start upwards from this spot. If you are creating your own maze, you must place a beginning and an end line in the maze creator.

2.      This is an ordinary wall. When you come from below, you are forced to turn either right or left.

Note: if you were to come at the obstacle from the right or left instead, you would not be presented the turn because you would have missed the obstacle.

3.     This is a dead end! In a hay-bales maze, you reverse directions when you hit one.

4.     This is a forced turn. You are presented only one direction when you hit it, so the maze does not pause.

Note: if you were to come at the obstacle from behind, this would be an ordinary wall with two choices.

5.     This blue line is a permeable wall. In addition to the two ordinary choices you have, you may also choose to continue forward.

6.     This is a permeable dead-end. Because the back of the dead-end is permeable, you may choose to turn around or continue forwards.

7.     This light green line is the end! Note: there can only be one beginning, but there may be multiple ends.

Features of the Electronic Maze

I have filled this maze with a number of features to make the game interface helpful to you. If you get lost in the menus, you can always return to the main menu by clicking the ‘Main Menu’ button on the left. This is a short list of a few features you might want to take note of:

  • Under the ‘Load Maze’ menu, there is an option to load a sample maze.
  • Maze Solver: this Main Menu button takes you the game interface, where you can solve the maze.
  • Refresh: this handy button on the left will erase your game-lines if it gets messy as you are solving the maze.
  • If you pause the maze (‘Stop the Maze’), you have the option to speed up or slow down the line motion.
  • Create Maze: this Main Menu button takes you to the game creator, where you can build and erase obstacles. To build an obstacle, select an obstacle type and click where you want it to go. To erase an obstacle, just click on the obstacle.

    • Other features can be found by exploring the interface. Have fun creating and solving mazes!

     

    About Hay-Bales Mazes

    The ‘hay-bales’ maze is a favorite childhood invention of mine. It feels more like a corn maze: you’renever quite sure where you’re going to end up! You can be on one side of the maze in one moment and on the other side in the next.

    I say it was a childhood invention, but it is clearly an adult game. A hay-bales maze is more difficult than a regular maze and takes more time to solve. It is like a puzzle. A hay-bales maze is to a regular maze as Sudoku is to counting on your fingers.

    I first thought of the idea after visiting Mount Vernon (George Washington’s home) as a child. At the plantation, there was a maze set up using hay bales. It was like a corn maze, except the hay bales were short and you could see where you were going. There was a catch: you were only allowed to turn when you hit a wall. Otherwise, you were required to always continue straight. After hitting a wall, you were allowed to choose which way to turn. The maze was difficult. I saw some children running through it doing what they wanted to and not following the simple maze rules. Some people would give up after trying for a minute, expecting a simple maze. I made it through, but it required me to explore a bit and to repeat my paths a few times.

    The maze fascinated me. It dawned on me that this maze could easily be recreated using graph paper in the same way that a corn maze could be ‘drawn’ on regular paper. I created the system of walls and turns that you see in my maze here. I also added a ‘permeable’ wall where you can also choose to go forward, just to make things more interesting.

    I started drawing ‘hay-bales’ mazes on graph paper and showing my friends. I saw the same result with the graph paper as I had seen in the maze at Mount Vernon: most people did not expect a complicated puzzle and would only spend a minute on it.

    Thus I have created a digital version of a Hay-Bales maze that makes it easier to make choices and easier to stay in-line on the graph paper. I think it is more fun this way, too, because you can see your line progressing on the page (I would not let people draw lines on my mazes).
